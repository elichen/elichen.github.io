Yen vs Dollar: Understanding Currency Strength

The Japanese Yen has generally been weaker than the US Dollar in recent years. This page explains the key factors behind this trend and provides a historical context for understanding currency dynamics between these two major economies.

Current Exchange Rate

As of April 2024, 1 US Dollar is worth approximately 150 Japanese Yen. This rate fluctuates daily based on various economic factors.

JPY per 1 USD (2014-2024)

Key Factors Influencing Yen Weakness

1. Monetary Policy Divergence

The Bank of Japan has maintained ultra-low interest rates and continued quantitative easing, while the US Federal Reserve has been raising interest rates. This divergence makes the Dollar more attractive for investors seeking higher yields.

2. Economic Growth Differences

The US economy has shown stronger growth compared to Japan's in recent years. Stronger economic growth typically leads to a stronger currency.

3. Inflation Rates

Japan has struggled with deflation or very low inflation for decades, while the US has seen higher inflation rates. Higher inflation in the US has led to expectations of tighter monetary policy, supporting the Dollar.

4. Trade Balance

Japan's trade balance has been affected by increased energy imports following the Fukushima disaster. This has put pressure on the Yen.

5. Safe-Haven Status

While both currencies are considered safe-havens, the Dollar often sees more inflows during global uncertainties due to its status as the world's primary reserve currency.

Historical Context: Yen-Dollar Exchange Rate Timeline

1971

End of Bretton Woods system. Yen begins to float against the Dollar.

1985

Plaza Accord leads to Yen appreciation against the Dollar.

1995

Yen reaches post-war high of 79.75 per Dollar.

2002

Japan's "Lost Decade" continues, keeping Yen weak.

2011

Fukushima disaster leads to Yen appreciation due to expectation of repatriation flows.

2012

Shinzo Abe elected, introduces "Abenomics" leading to Yen depreciation.

2020-2024

COVID-19 pandemic and subsequent recovery. Diverging monetary policies lead to further Yen weakness.

Conclusion

The relative weakness of the Yen against the Dollar is a result of complex economic factors and policy decisions over time. While short-term fluctuations occur, the long-term trend has been influenced by diverging monetary policies, economic growth patterns, and global economic events. Understanding these factors provides insight into not just the Yen-Dollar dynamic, but also broader principles of currency valuation and international economics.
